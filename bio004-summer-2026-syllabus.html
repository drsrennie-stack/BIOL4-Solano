Course at a Glance

This is an 8-week summer intensive. The course covers the same content, depth, and rigor as the 16-week semester format, compressed into half the time. Each class day represents roughly two days of regular semester instruction. Plan accordingly.

Class meets four days per week (Monday through Thursday), with both lecture and lab on the same day. Plan on approximately 20 hours per week of outside-class study to maintain pace, more if you are aiming for an A.

The summer compact

Because of the compressed pace, three things matter more than usual:

  1. Show up prepared. Pre-work is the foundation of TBL, and TBL is 40% of your grade. Walking in unprepared will cost you points immediately, before you even sit down.
  2. Don't fall behind. You cannot catch up in this course the way you might in a regular semester. One missed day is roughly two missed days of content.
  3. Use lab time well. Lab is your structured, forward-moving study time. Treat it as study time, not social time.

Course Description

A study of the structural organization of the human body, from cellular to organismal level. Throughout the course, various types of instruction are used, including microscopic investigation of prepared slides of tissues and organs, gross (macroscopic) anatomical dissection, and examination of prosected human material.

Hours: 48 to 54 lecture, 96 to 108 lab. This course is primarily intended for nursing, allied health, kinesiology, and other health-related majors. (Formerly BIO 006.)

Prerequisites & advisories

  • Prerequisites: None
  • Co-requisites: None
  • Advisories: SCC minimum Math and English standards
  • Recommended: Satisfactory completion of BIO 16 (Introduction to Human Biology) and BIO 16L (Human Biology Laboratory) is strongly recommended but not required.

Student Learning Outcomes

Upon successful completion of the course, students should be able to:

  1. Identify and distinguish tissues based on currently recognized criteria.
  2. Describe the basic microscopic and gross anatomy of the major systems of the human body.
  3. Identify, in the laboratory, major microscopic and gross structures of the major systems of the human body.

Course Workload Expectations

This is a 5-unit course (3 lecture units, 2 lab units) delivered in 8 weeks. Per the Carnegie Unit standard (2 hours of outside study per credit hour per week), applied to both lecture and lab credits because anatomy lab is memorization-intensive study (atlas drilling, structure identification, lab practical preparation) rather than wet-lab experimentation that completes itself in the lab session, plan on approximately 20 hours per week of outside-class study to maintain pace at a passing-grade level (C). Students aiming for higher grades typically invest 25 or more hours per week, depending on prior exposure to the material, study skills, and preparation.

Be realistic about your current commitments. Consider your other courses, employment, and personal responsibilities, and evaluate where additional study time could realistically come from without making changes that could negatively affect your overall well-being.

If your schedule does not allow for this level of sustained effort, the 8-week intensive may not be the right fit this term. Proceeding carefully through foundational courses is often more beneficial than rushing. Repeating this course or physiology due to insufficient preparation creates greater delays in your academic progress than planning a manageable schedule from the outset.

Homework & success strategy

Homework is recommended, not always required or graded. It is designed to reinforce your learning and improve exam performance.

Prioritize: high lab exam scores, strong TBL performance, and consistent end-of-day lab quiz performance. Homework is minimal and intentionally structured to support mastery, not to pad your grade.

  • Pros: Fewer mandatory tasks let you focus on high-impact studying. Missing a non-graded assignment will not significantly hurt your grade.
  • Cons: If you are used to earning points from low-effort tasks, that strategy will not work here. Your competence is measured directly through assessments and TBL.

Bottom line: There are no shortcuts. Focus on learning the material deeply, performing well on exams and team work, and becoming truly competent in the content.

Assessment Details

Team-Based Learning (TBL)

TBL assessments link course material to clinical problems and concepts. They encourage consistent preparation and active engagement with problem-based learning. Each TBL focuses on a clinical case that integrates the anatomy of the week.

Each TBL has two graded components

  • iRAT (Individual Readiness Assessment Test): a 10 to 15 question individual quiz assessing basic preparation. Weighted 3x.
  • tRAT (Team Readiness Assessment Test): the same quiz, taken with your team. Weighted 1x.

Following the tRAT, students work collaboratively on problem-solving and application questions. Discussion is strongly encouraged. These application questions are not graded, in order to encourage open discussion. However, every student is expected to understand and be able to explain the rationale for application answers if called upon.

Scoring

  • Each quiz is 10 points.
  • iRAT: 10 points x 3 = 30 points
  • tRAT: 10 points x 1 = 10 points
  • Total possible per TBL: 40 points

Example

  • iRAT: 7 of 10 = 21 points
  • tRAT: 9 of 10 = 9 points
  • Total: 30 of 40 = 75%

This weighting heavily emphasizes individual performance to encourage personal preparation rather than reliance on team members. The iRAT alone accounts for 75% of your TBL grade. Most teams score 90% or higher on the tRAT, so your individual iRAT scores largely determine your final TBL outcome.

TBL assessments cannot be made up. No exceptions. The lowest TBL score will be dropped at the end of the term. Use this drop wisely.
Lab Practical Exams

There are 7 lab practical exams: 6 module exams plus 1 cumulative lab exam at the end of the term. Exams consist of short answer and fill-in-the-blank questions, in which you will identify anatomy from microscope slides, pictures, charts, anatomical models, and human dissections.

Format

  • Approximately 50 to 60 questions per exam
  • You rotate through stations on a timer
  • Each station has 2 questions
  • You have 90 seconds per station
  • Remain at each station until told to move

Exam conduct

  • Spelling counts. Bring your favorite pen(s). Pencils are not permitted.
  • Do not look around the room or at other stations while at your station.
  • Fold your paper in half so your answers are covered. Look at the wall or the table.
  • Failure to comply will result in collection of your exam, graded as is.
  • No makeup lab practicals will be given.
Cumulative Lab Exam

During the final week of the term, you will complete one cumulative lab exam, which counts as your seventh lab exam grade.

Partial grade improvement

The cumulative exam provides an opportunity for partial grade improvement on previous module lab exams. If your score on the cumulative is higher than your score on a prior module lab exam, I will apply 50% of the improvement to the corresponding earlier exam. The original exam grade is not replaced.

Example

If you earned a 40% on Lab Exam 1 and an 80% on the cumulative lab exam, the improvement is 40 percentage points. Half of that improvement (20 points) is added to your original score, raising it to 60%.

The cumulative exam includes equal representation of material from each module and is comprehensive. This structure is intentional and is designed to ensure continued engagement with all course content.

End-of-Day Lab Quizzes

End-of-day lab quizzes use a community accountability format. The mechanism is intentionally designed to make lab time a real collaborative work session, not parallel solo study.

How it works

  • At the end of nearly every lab session, I ask one person in one randomly selected group a single quiz question covering material from that day's lab period.
  • The entire class earns the same score based on that one student's answer. Each quiz is worth 3 points.
  • You do not know in advance who will be called on or which group will be selected.

What this means for you

Your lab quiz grade depends on the readiness of every student in the room, not just yourself. If everyone uses lab time well, the selected student knows the answer and the class earns full credit. If any student has not engaged with the material, that student could be the one called on, and the whole class loses points.

Show up for each other. Put the work in during lab. Help your group members understand the material as you go. This mechanism rewards collective preparation and penalizes isolated effort. Lab time is collaborative time by design.

Attendance and scoring

  • Students present during a lab quiz earn the class score for that quiz.
  • Students absent during a lab quiz earn a zero for that quiz, per the no-makeup attendance policy.
  • There are no dropped lab quiz scores at the end of the term.
Depth of Knowledge (DOK) levels

My goal is to help you learn anatomy in a way that prepares you for real-world healthcare applications, not just memorization. Exam and assignment questions are structured using different Depth of Knowledge levels. Most assessment questions will be DOK 2 and DOK 3.

Level Type How you will use it Weight
DOK 1 Basic recall Naming structures, defining terms, simple labeling. Helpful for learning the material early on. ~10%
DOK 2 Skills & concepts Applying knowledge to identify relationships, structures in context, or interpreting images. ~40 to 50%
DOK 3 Strategic thinking Solving clinical problems, justifying answers, predicting the impact of injuries. Requires explanation and reasoning. ~40 to 50%
DOK 4 Extended thinking Optional in this course. Used in bonus, advanced, or optional integrative projects across systems. ~0 to 10%

Additional bonus points

In addition to the 3.0% scholar bonus, you may earn up to 25 additional scholar points added to your final point total through Community Rounds participation: hosting or attending peer-led review sessions (see below for the mechanism).

No additional extra credit will be given in this course. Scholar Points are your only path to any extra credit. Please do not ask. No exceptions.

Community Rounds, peer-led learning

Community Rounds are student-organized review sessions that you run for each other outside of class time. Examples: a peer-led Zoom session drilling spiral lab ID, a Saturday Kahoot game covering recent module content, a small study circle working through TBL prep together. The goal is to build the kind of mutual support that healthcare programs depend on, while reinforcing material through teaching and active recall.

This is fully peer-organized. I do not schedule, host, or attend these sessions. Students design them, invite each other, and run them. The Canvas "Community Rounds" discussion thread is the central record.

How it works

  • Hosting: Post your session in the Canvas "Community Rounds" thread at least 24 hours in advance, with the date, format (Zoom, Kahoot, in-person), topic, and how to join.
  • After hosting: Reply to your own announcement post with a short session log: who attended (names or count), what was covered, and any resource link (Kahoot code, slide link, etc.).
  • Attending: Reply in the announcement thread after the session: "Attended on [date]. One thing I'll remember: [one sentence]."
  • Sessions must be open to the class (posted to the thread). Private group sessions do not qualify.

Points

  • Hosting a Community Round: 3 points per session
  • Attending a Community Round: 1 point per session
  • Counted toward the 25 point supplemental pool described above.

The Canvas thread is the artifact. I review it at the end of the term and total the points. No separate forms, no logs to submit. Run sessions, post in the thread, get credit.

Important notes. Scholar Points are not retroactive and cannot replace missed assignments or exams. They are earned through both intentional effort and sustained academic performance. The maximum bonus is 3.0%, applied at the end of the term. You will see it reflected in your final grade, but it will not show on Canvas.

AI Policy

The World Economic Forum defines generative AI as "a category of artificial intelligence (AI) algorithms that generate new outputs based on the data they have been trained on. Unlike traditional AI systems that are designed to recognize patterns and make predictions, generative AI creates new content in the form of images, text, audio, and more." I may use AI detection tools to screen student work in this class. Please discuss any questions or concerns about these tools with me.

When AI is allowed

You will be taught some powerful ways to use AI to your advantage in this course. There will be occasional assignments that you can complete using AI. They will be designated as such in the assignment title or details. You may use AI to help with tutoring yourself or creating study sheets, and I highly recommend you do so.

When AI is not allowed

  • You may only use AI for AI-designated graded assignments and anything else that is not graded.
  • You may not use AI for exams or graded portions of TBL.
  • Any plagiarism or AI-detection rating on graded assignments above 25% will earn an automatic zero.
  • If you are unsure whether a tool or website is a generative AI tool, or whether it is permitted on a specific assignment, contact me for clarification before submitting your work.

See the AI Honor Contract for additional requirements.

Tips for Success

Anatomy is a challenging course, and the 8-week intensive amplifies that. There is no sugar-coating it. By the end, you will be amazed at how much you have learned and how far your critical thinking and study skills have come. Expect obstacles, and know that you can overcome them with the right strategies.

This is not a passive class

To succeed, you need more than watching lectures and skimming the textbook. Passive learning is not enough, especially in summer. Students who treat anatomy like a lecture-based memorize-and-regurgitate course often find themselves surprised by exam scores that do not match their effort.

The truth: Success requires active engagement, consistent effort, and strategic study methods.

Build & use learning tools

Be creative. Be resourceful. Use the tools I provide and learn how to build your own. Successful students often make:

  • Color-coded diagrams
  • Concept maps
  • Flashcards and apps like Anki
  • Study sheets that compare and contrast structures or processes
  • Self-quizzes or practice questions they design themselves

The secret sauce: less prep, more recall

Memorization matters in anatomy, but visuals matter too. Recall is the goal. Too many students spend 90% of their study time preparing (retyping notes, organizing folders, rewatching lectures) and only 10% testing themselves.

Recommended rule: 20% prep, 80% active recall. If your study time is not giving you the grades you expect, examine how you are spending that time. Quantity matters, but quality matters more.

Learn how to learn

If you are struggling, do not just try harder, try smarter. Use the learning resources posted in Canvas. Explore how successful learners study when the material is fast-paced and high-stakes. That is a skill that will serve you in this course and in every professional program that follows.

Attitude & accountability

A positive, respectful attitude goes a long way. Avoid complaining about the class, the professor, the material, or your group. It erodes morale, yours and others'.

  • Stay solution-focused.
  • Be respectful of your peers' time and contributions.
  • Carry your weight in group activities.
  • Support one another, and expect the same in return.
  • Stop negative, unproductive talk when you hear it.

You will spend the next 8 weeks learning together. Treat each other like teammates, because that is what you are.

Your grade does not define your worth

Let me be clear: your grade is feedback, not a verdict. It is one data point in your journey, not the sum of who you are. If you fall short of your expectations, do not give up. Adjust your approach, ask for help, and try again.

Comparison is the enemy of progress. Focus on your growth.

You belong here. Keep showing up, and keep going. I am here to support you. Your crew is here for you too.
